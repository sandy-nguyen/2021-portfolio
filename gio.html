The ASD Digital Coordinator
Gio

An opensource digital coordinator for ASD parents that aims to shape the caretaking experience through journaling, insights, and tracking.

Medical terminology used throughout this case study:
Autism Spectrum
Disorder (ASD)

A neurological and developmental disorder that begins early in childhood and lasts throughout a person's life. It affects how a person acts and interacts with others, communicates, and learns.

Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA)

Applied Behavior Analysis is the process of studying behavior in order to put into place appropriate behavioral interventions.

An individual who is trained to provide and supervise behavior analysis.

Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)

A federal law that required the creation of national standards to protect sensitive patient health information from being disclosed without the patient's consent or knowledge.

Designing for impact

I spent my 2020 summer at Goinvo working on Gio, an app that aims to help ASD caregivers through digital coordination and behavior tracking.

Goinvo had previously ideated on this project back in 2018, but never got to finishing it. Jinghan (the other intern) and I decided to pick up from their previous work because it was a complex problem that we were passionate about. The agency is currently in the process of gathering funding for this concept.

Problem statement

In the field of Autism research and study, parents’ data input is considered somewhat not reliable or trustworthy in the clinician’s perspective. Due to its inconsistency, there is a lack of training and enormous stress placed on the parents in the caregiving journey. However, parental involvement is extremely important for the progression of skills from ABA therapy sessions to more natural settings, and for the success of the child receiving ABA. Several studies have found that children are more successful when parents are consistently involved in the treatment (Ozonoff & Cathcart, 1998).

This collective effort requires the care team to assess the child, come up with a treatment plan, collect data, use data as guidance for therapy, and intervention to reassess. Therefore, we should design a consistent, efficient, and understandable way to help parents collect their ASD child’s data, so that they can contribute insights to their child’s treatment, understand their child better, and become a better advocate for their child’s autism.

Taking a look at
Goinvo’s past work
Secondary research

Goinvo’s previous work was extremely well researched and served as a great foundation for our app. However, it was last worked on in 2018, and so the designs were extremely outdated. Before I could pick up from where they had left off, I wanted to first understand Autism and the complexity of the disorder from different spectrums.

I read and highlighted the secondary research done in the studio, reviewed academic papers, watched ABA training videos, parent interview videos, and even documentaries on how people with autism live their daily lives and interact with their providers/caregivers. We looked at competitor apps (for ex. Birdbox), but there was nothing on the current market that alligned with our problem scope.

We also read The New York Times Understanding Autism magazine religiously. This special issue covers topics ranging from child and family, autism and science, aiding the autistic, and autism in our culture. Articles and testimonials from various stakeholders helped us to understand and explore the problem scope.

Together, these secondary research sources helped us build a solid foundation for our design and gave us the essential background knowledge that helped us make interview protocols for our primary research.

We also constructed an impact map that connected our goal, stakeholders, problems, ideas, and impact together. From there, we found three major painpoints:

Understand

It was hard for parents to understand their child’s autism and there was a lack of access to educational resources.

Track

Parents were not able to act upon skill deficits and record major incidents conveniently and consistently.

Learn

Parents struggled with analyzing their child’s behavior digitally.

With a clearly defined problem scope and a specific user group in mind, we started our design session by brainstorming and sketching. We came up with around 50 rough sketches, clustered our top picked ideas, and used them to compose potential app features. Then we generated a concept architecture map to guide our design. Because ASD is on a broad spectrum, we decided to develop a specific user scenario, focusing on a parent named Jessica, who has a child with autism named Lucas.

Ideation
Tap me!
User scenario/persona:
Jessica White
ASD parent, Freelance Writer
BACKGROUND

Jessica's 6 year old son Lucas was recently diagnosed with high functioning ASD. Although it was hard for the family to accept Lucas's diagnosis, the family have decided to get Lucas to do therapy as soon as possible so Lucas can gain more social skills. Lucas's doctor had suggested ABA therapy for the family to try out. Jessica took their son to a local ABA therapy center. After the waiting period, the BCBA Erin was able to assess Lucas on his behavior and performance.

GOALS

- Get Lucas to put proper clothes for the weather in the morning by himself

- Help Lucas build positive eating habits without inducing problematic behavior

- Achieve a better
understanding of Autism as a whole

PAIN POINTS

- Lucas has a certain fixation on specific clothing

- Lucas only eats chicken nuggets without throwing a tantrum

- Has a hard time handing off caretaking experience to husband (John) when he arrives home from work

User story map
Tap me!
Understanding our users
(primary research)

With our refined interview protcols finished, we recruited six stakeholders including a board certified behavior analyst (BCBA), behavior interventionist, special education teacher, registered nurse, ASD clinic IT administrator, and ASD parent for 45 minute semi-structured Interviews over Zoom. We then recorded, transcribed, and highlighted the key insights from the interview on sticky notes in Miro. The notes were clustered based on recurring themes in Excel, and ranked using metrics we designed.

I also created a Google survey questionnaire to respect the privacy of participants that wanted to stay anonymous.

With our specific user scenario made, we then created a user story map that helped us connect our goal, stakeholders, problems, ideas, and impact together to illustrate where all the magic moments of the app would happen. In design there are these things called “magic moments” which will make your product seamless and unique from others. It is our mission as designers to find those magic moments and bring them to life.

In the context of our app, these were the moments where insights would be generated by our algorithim, whether it be curated courses, therapy recommendations, or even social determinates of health insights that gave the parents a better understanding of how to be a better advocate for their child.

User task flow & wireframes:
Tap me!
Color exploration & typography

We chose orange because it represented encouragement, enthusiasm, and determination. Since purple is a bit of a cooler tone, we decided to make this our base color because it balanced out the bright orange and complimented the scheme well. We wanted to be wary of color contrast ratios because of WCAG accesibility standards and were mindful of this when choosing our surfaces, text, and line colors. As for our typography, we chose the sans serif font Gilroy since it was a modern font with a geometric touch. The geometric font paired nicely with our flat design, and had flexible font weights.

Furthermore, I made a parent task flow to show how Jessica would use this app in her scenario and to display the detailed interactions that would help us ideate wireframes. After reviewing our user task flow for the document management system feature, we decided to eliminate it. We wanted to make sure we would design the feature when we had more research on privacy. Our initial idea was to use a google drive integration, but their system’s privacy is not ideal because once a file is deleted there are still traces of it in the system. (Google is currently working on fixing this!)

Before moving to the final design, we scheduled a meeting with our parent participants to review our wireframes. They gave us a lot of good feedback and raised some concerns about privacy regarding our fourth module of the concept map—the connect with others/sharing profile feature. We took her insights, and iterated on our wireframes. For the final design, we focused on designing the screens for the key tasks that we conjured up in our storyboards, and using Jessica’s scenario, we selected the several screens below to show the key features of each task.

This feature allows parents to create a routine for their child and act upon skill deficits/problematic behaviors by tracking behavior goals suggested by therapists at home. This tracked data overtime can create insights and explanations for certain behaviors. In Jessica’s case, she would use this to track whether or not Lucas has been succeeding in his morning routine by making sure he completes his nested mini tasks and checking if there are any improvements in behavior.

Final design
Target behavior task tracking

The journal entry feature allows parents to record major incidents/outbursts, and their potential cause factors to construct a more complete health picture for their child using the ABC’s of ABA therapy. For example, this might help Jessica record any actions that help Lucas stop his outburts effectively. (Calling Lucas’ favorite teacher helps him calm down when he is self harming himself.)

Journal entry

The data analytics section is where Jessica will be able to see auto populated insights based off of the data she collects from Lucas’ behavior. This is where she can learn from the patterns of repeated behavior and share a report of her findings to the BCBA if needed as well.

Data analytics

Initially, the child’s profile was supposed to have the fourth module that allowed users to connect with other patients with similar profiles. However, we felt that we needed to work on a better flow for privacy and chose to hold off on this feature until we could better visualize a solution for this regarding HIPAA. The child’s profile enables the parent to add users to the caregiving team, and access the insight center shown above.

Child’s profile

The resource center is a education hub that gives parents and patients the ability to learn from curated resources based off tracked data. We wanted to make this as engaging as possible, so there are three different types of resources provided. First, there are articles and short posts that parents can read for quick information. Secondly, there are interactive videos that users can watch. Lastly, we created an interactive dialogue course for parents to feel engaged in the learning process.

Resource center
Medicine tracker

Our final feature was the medicine tracker. This allows Jessica to track and record all of Lucas’ medication side effects in one place. We also integrated a section that gave generic recommendations. This was to give low income parents information on alternative cost efficient medicines.

Understand

The education center feature helps parents understand about their child’s autism.

Track

Parents are now able to act upon skill deficits and record major incidents conveniently and consistently.

Learn

Parents can now learn about their child’s behavior patterns through data analytics and generated insights.

Conclusion & future steps

All in all, in the future we plan to build out a communication feature that allows providers and caretakers to exchange health information regarding the child (with HIPAA compliance of course)! A future documentation management system would be something to consider as well. We might also explore an ASD child profile matching feature to create a networking aspect and build communities that will empower each other. Furthermore, we want to come up with guidelines to potentially use this model to adapt for other special needs. One constraint Gio has is that this app is still created with a specific scenario in mind (Jessica’s). I want to interview more parents to explore the unmet needs of children on the spectrum, and potentially co-design with them. The clinician’s app view needs to be ideated too. We plan to interview ASD children, since at some point, the data that is being collected will become theirs as they become adults. One team member is working on this for her thesis to continue the project. More to come!

Thank you!

Designing for impact

I spent my 2020 summer at Goinvo working on Gio, an app that aims to help ASD caregivers through digital coordination and behavior tracking.

Goinvo had previously ideated on this project back in 2018, but never got to finishing it. Jinghan (the other intern) and I decided to pick up from their previous work because it was a complex problem that we were passionate about. The agency is currently in the process of gathering funding for this concept.

Medical terminology used throughout this case study:
Autism Spectrum
Disorder (ASD)

A neurological and developmental disorder that begins early in childhood and lasts throughout a person's life. It affects how a person acts and interacts with others, communicates, and learns.

Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA)

Applied Behavior Analysis is the process of studying behavior in order to put into place appropriate behavioral interventions.

An individual who is trained to provide and supervise behavior analysis.

Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA)

A federal law that required the creation of national standards to protect sensitive patient health information from being disclosed without the patient's consent or knowledge.

Problem statement

In the field of Autism research and study, parents’ data input is considered somewhat not reliable or trustworthy in the clinician’s perspective. Due to its inconsistency, there is a lack of training and enormous stress placed on the parents in the caregiving journey. However, parental involvement is extremely important for the progression of skills from ABA therapy sessions to more natural settings, and for the success of the child receiving ABA. Several studies have found that children are more successful when parents are consistently involved in the treatment (Ozonoff & Cathcart, 1998).

This collective effort requires the care team to assess the child, come up with a treatment plan, collect data, use data as guidance for therapy, and intervention to reassess. Therefore, we should design a consistent, efficient, and understandable way to help parents collect their ASD child’s data, so that they can contribute insights to their child’s treatment, understand their child better, and become a better advocate for their child’s autism.

Summarized insights:

Goinvo’s previous work was extremely well researched and served as a great foundation for our app. However, it was last worked on in 2018, and so the designs were extremely outdated. Before I could pick up from where they had left off, I wanted to first understand Autism and the complexity of the disorder from different spectrums.

I read and highlighted the secondary research done in the studio, reviewed academic papers, watched ABA training videos, parent interview videos, and even documentaries on how people with autism live their daily lives and interact with their providers/caregivers. We looked at competitor apps (for ex. Birdbox), but there was nothing on the current market that alligned with our problem scope.

We also read The New York Times Understanding Autism magazine religiously. This special issue covers topics ranging from child and family, autism and science, aiding the autistic, and autism in our culture. Articles and testimonials from various stakeholders helped us to understand and explore the problem scope.

Together, these secondary research sources helped us build a solid foundation for our design and gave us the essential background knowledge that helped us make interview protocols for our primary research.

With our refined interview protcols finished, we recruited six stakeholders including a board certified behavior analyst (BCBA), behavior interventionist, special education teacher, registered nurse, ASD clinic IT administrator, and ASD parent for 45 minute semi-structured Interviews over Zoom. We then recorded, transcribed, and highlighted the key insights from the interview on sticky notes in Miro. The notes were clustered based on recurring themes in Excel, and ranked using metrics we designed.

I also created a Google survey questionnaire to respect the privacy of participants that wanted to stay anonymous.

Jessica's 6 year old son Lucas was recently diagnosed with high functioning ASD. Lucas's doctor had suggested ABA therapy for the family to try out. Jessica took their son to a local ABA therapy center. After the waiting period, the BCBA Erin was able to assess Lucas on his behavior and performance.
